Introduction to Day Trading

Everything you need to understand what day trading actually is — before touching a chart. The mindset, the learning curve, and why this is nothing like what you see on social media.

📅

What is Day Trading?

Click to read
▼

Day trading is the practice of buying and selling financial instruments — currencies, stocks, indices, or commodities — within the same trading session. Unlike a traditional investor who might hold shares for months or years and profit only if the price rises, a day trader opens and closes their positions within hours, sometimes minutes.

One of the most important things to understand early on is that trading is not just about buying low and selling high. In the markets, you can also sell first and buy back later — known as going short. This means you can potentially profit whether the market is rising or falling. If you believe a currency pair is about to drop in value, you sell it, and if you're right, you profit from that decline. This is a fundamental difference from simply owning shares and hoping they go up.

It's worth being honest from the outset: day trading is not a shortcut to wealth, and it is not gambling — though it can resemble the latter if approached without discipline. The traders who succeed treat it as a skill, developed carefully over time, with risk management at its core.

Beginner
🧠

The Psychology of Trading

Click to read
▼

If there is one thing that separates consistently profitable traders from everyone else, it is rarely the strategy — it is the mindset. Fear and greed are the two great enemies of a trader. Fear causes you to exit a winning trade too early, or to hesitate on a valid entry because your last trade was a loss. Greed causes you to hold on too long, or to risk more than you should.

Losses are part of the job. Even the best traders have losing trades — sometimes losing days and weeks. What matters is that your winners, over time, outweigh your losers. A trader with a 50% win rate can still be very profitable if winning trades consistently make more than losing ones give back.

Never risk more than you can afford to lose emotionally. There is a specific amount of money at which each person stops thinking clearly. Risking more than that on a single trade is a fast road to poor decisions. Start small, stay consistent.

Discipline over excitement. The most dangerous days are often the most exciting ones — fast-moving markets, high volatility, the urge to jump in. The best traders wait patiently for their setup and walk away from anything that doesn't meet their criteria.

The Realistic Learning Curve

Click to read
▼

Learning to trade consistently and profitably takes time. For most people, that means one to three years of serious, structured practice before anything resembling consistent results emerges. There is no shortcut, and anyone telling you otherwise is either selling something or deeply lucky.

Stage 1 — The Excitement Phase. Everything feels possible. You open a demo account, make some trades that go well, and start wondering why everyone doesn't do this. Early success in demo can create dangerous false confidence.

Stage 2 — The Humbling. Real money is involved and emotions kick in. Losses feel larger than they are. This is where most people quit — and also where the real learning begins.

Stage 3 — The Grind. You develop a strategy, test it, break it and rebuild it. Progress is slow and rarely linear. This stage can last a long time.

Stage 4 — Consistency. With enough screen time and honest self-reflection, patterns emerge. Losses stop feeling like failures and start feeling like data. The traders who make it are not the most talented — they are the most patient.

Introduction to Forex

The foreign exchange market is the largest in the world. Here's what that means for you as a UK-based trader — and why it suits day trading particularly well.

🌍

What is the Forex Market?

Click to read
▼

Forex — short for foreign exchange — is the global market where currencies are bought and sold. Every time a business imports goods from another country, or a traveller exchanges money at an airport, they are participating in the Forex market in some form.

Currencies are always traded in pairs — for example, GBP/USD (British Pound vs US Dollar) or EUR/JPY (Euro vs Japanese Yen). When you trade a pair, you are simultaneously buying one currency and selling the other. If you think the Pound will strengthen against the Dollar, you buy GBP/USD. If it rises, you profit. If it falls, you make a loss.

The Forex market is the largest financial market in the world, with over $7 trillion traded every single day. That scale means it is extremely liquid — you can almost always get in and out of a trade quickly, which is exactly what day traders need.

Beginner
🕐

Market Sessions & Hours

Click to read
▼

Unlike stock markets that open and close at fixed times, the Forex market runs 24 hours a day, five days a week. It follows the sun around the world across three main sessions:

Tokyo Session (midnight–9am UK time) — Generally quieter, with most activity in Japanese Yen pairs. Useful for those who trade early mornings.

London Session (8am–4pm UK time) — The most active session for UK-based traders and one of the most important globally. London handles a huge proportion of daily Forex volume, meaning tighter spreads and more opportunity.

New York Session (1pm–10pm UK time) — Overlaps with London from 1pm–4pm, creating the most volatile and high-volume window of the trading day. Major price moves often happen here.

As a UK-based trader, the London open and the London/New York overlap are typically the best windows to focus on.

📖

Key Concepts: Pips, Lots & Leverage

Click to read
▼

These are the core terms you'll encounter constantly. Understanding them is essential before placing any trade.

Pip — A pip (short for "percentage in point") is the standard unit of price movement in Forex. For most currency pairs, one pip is a move of 0.0001 — the fourth decimal place. If GBP/USD moves from 1.25000 to 1.25010, that is one pip. However, there is a smaller unit beneath the pip: the point (sometimes called a pipette), which is one tenth of a pip, represented by the fifth decimal place. There are ten points in one pip. This matters in practice because the spread — the difference between the buy and sell price — is typically quoted in points rather than full pips. A spread of "3 points" is 0.3 of a pip, not three full pips.

Spread — The difference between the Bid (sell) price and the Ask (buy) price, quoted in points. This is effectively the broker's fee on every trade. Tighter spreads mean lower trading costs, which is one reason major pairs are preferred by beginners.

Lot — The size of your trade. A standard lot is 100,000 units of currency. Beginners typically use micro lots (1,000 units) to keep risk very small while learning.

Leverage — Borrowed capital that lets you control a larger position than your deposit. Leverage of 1:30 means £1,000 controls £30,000. It amplifies both profits and losses, so it must be used carefully.

Long vs Short — Going long means buying, expecting the price to rise. Going short means selling first, expecting the price to fall — and buying back later at a lower price to profit.

Beginner
💱

Major, Minor & Exotic Pairs

Click to read
▼

Not all currency pairs are created equal. They are grouped into three categories based on how widely traded they are.

Major pairs always include the US Dollar and are the most liquid and most traded in the world. Examples: GBP/USD, EUR/USD, USD/JPY, USD/CHF. These have the tightest spreads and the most reliable price movement — ideal for beginners.

Minor pairs (also called cross pairs) don't include the Dollar but feature other major currencies. Examples: EUR/GBP, GBP/JPY, EUR/AUD. These can be good for experienced traders but can be more unpredictable.

Exotic pairs combine a major currency with one from an emerging economy — for example USD/TRY (Turkish Lira) or USD/ZAR (South African Rand). These have wide spreads, lower liquidity, and higher risk. Best avoided until you have considerable experience.

As a beginner, stick to major pairs — particularly GBP/USD and EUR/USD. They are well-documented, highly liquid, and behave more predictably than anything else.

Forex Glossary — Key Terms Explained

Click to open — bookmark this for reference
▼

These are the terms you are most likely to encounter as a beginner. Come back to this card whenever you hit an unfamiliar word.

Ask Price — The price at which your broker will sell a currency pair to you. The price you pay when entering a Buy (Long) trade.

Bid Price — The price at which your broker will buy a currency pair from you. The price you receive when entering a Sell (Short) trade.

Spread — The difference between the Bid and Ask price, measured in points. This is the broker's fee on each trade. A tighter spread means a lower cost to trade.

Pip — The standard unit of price movement, equal to 0.0001 for most pairs (the fourth decimal place). A move from 1.25000 to 1.25010 is one pip.

Point (Pipette) — One tenth of a pip, equal to 0.00001 (the fifth decimal place). Spreads are typically quoted in points. Ten points make one pip.

Lot — The unit used to measure trade size. A standard lot is 100,000 units of the base currency. A mini lot is 10,000 and a micro lot is 1,000. Beginners should start with micro lots.

Leverage — Borrowed capital from your broker that lets you control a position larger than your deposit. 1:30 leverage means £1,000 controls £30,000. Magnifies both profits and losses.

Margin — The amount of your own money required to open and hold a leveraged trade. Think of it as a deposit held by your broker while the trade is open.

Long — To go Long means to buy a currency pair, expecting its value to rise. You profit if the price goes up.

Short — To go Short means to sell a currency pair first, expecting its value to fall, then buy back at a lower price to profit.

Base Currency — The first currency in a pair. In GBP/USD, the Pound (GBP) is the base currency. When you buy the pair, you are buying the base currency.

Quote Currency — The second currency in a pair. In GBP/USD, the Dollar (USD) is the quote currency. The price tells you how many units of the quote currency one unit of the base is worth.

Bull Market — A market trending upwards. A trader expecting prices to rise is said to be "bullish."

Bear Market — A market trending downwards. A trader expecting prices to fall is said to be "bearish."

Stop Loss — An instruction to automatically close a trade if the price moves against you by a set amount. An essential risk management tool that limits how much you can lose on any single trade.

Take Profit — An instruction to automatically close a trade when the price reaches a target profit level. Locks in your gain without requiring you to watch the screen.

Market Order — An instruction to buy or sell immediately at the current market price.

Pending Order — An instruction to open a trade only when the price reaches a level you specify in advance. Useful for entering at a precise price without watching the screen.

Support Level — A price level where a pair has historically had difficulty falling below. Traders often look to buy near support, expecting a bounce upwards.

Resistance Level — A price level where a pair has historically had difficulty rising above. Traders often look to sell near resistance, expecting a reversal downwards.

Candlestick — A chart bar showing the open, high, low, and close price for a given time period. The most widely used chart type in Forex. Covered in detail in the MT5 section.

Timeframe — The period each candlestick represents. Common timeframes: 1 minute (M1), 5 minutes (M5), 1 hour (H1), 4 hours (H4), daily (D1). Shorter timeframes show more detail; longer ones show broader trends.

Trend — The general direction a currency pair is moving over time. "The trend is your friend" — trading in the direction of the prevailing trend is generally safer than against it.

Volatility — How much and how quickly a price moves. High volatility means larger swings and greater potential profit — but also greater risk.

Liquidity — How easily a pair can be bought or sold without significantly affecting its price. Major pairs are highly liquid; exotic pairs are not.

Slippage — The difference between the price you expected when placing an order and the price at which it was actually executed. More common during high-volatility news events.

Swap / Rollover — A fee (or occasionally a credit) applied when a trade is held open overnight. Reflects the interest rate difference between the two currencies in the pair.

Risk/Reward Ratio — A comparison of potential profit to potential loss on a trade. A 1:2 ratio means risking one unit to potentially gain two. Consistently trading with a favourable ratio is a cornerstone of profitable trading.

Drawdown — The reduction in your account balance from its peak to its lowest point over a period. A key personal performance measure and a critical rule in all prop firm accounts.

Equity — The current real-time value of your account including any open trades. If your balance is $10,000 and an open trade shows a $200 profit, your equity is $10,200.

Balance — The total in your account based only on closed trades. Does not reflect the value of any currently open positions — that is what equity is for.

Section 4

Prop Firms — Trade Big Without Big Capital

One of the most eye-opening things about modern trading: you don't need £50,000 to trade £50,000. Prop firms fund traders who can prove they have a consistent strategy.

🏦

What is a Prop Firm?

Click to read
▼

A proprietary trading firm — or "prop firm" — is a company that provides capital to traders, allowing them to trade with significantly more money than they could afford on their own. Rather than risking their money on unknown traders, most prop firms use a structured evaluation process to identify people who can trade consistently and responsibly before funding them.

The model works like this: you pay a relatively small one-off fee to take a trading challenge. If you pass, the firm allocates you a funded account — typically ranging from £10,000 to £200,000 or more. You then trade that account and keep a substantial share of any profits you make, often between 70% and 90%. The firm keeps the rest.

For traders who have developed a solid strategy but don't have large personal capital, this is genuinely game-changing. The barrier to trading at a meaningful level is no longer "do you have £50,000 sitting spare?" — it becomes "can you prove you know what you're doing?"

Key Concept
🎯

The Challenge / Evaluation

Click to read
▼

Before a prop firm will fund you, you must pass an evaluation — usually called a challenge. This is designed to prove that you can trade profitably while staying within strict risk rules. Think of it as a job interview, except the interview is a live trading test lasting several weeks.

A typical two-phase challenge works like this. In Phase 1, you are given a demo account and must hit a profit target — often around 8–10% — within a set number of days, without breaching any risk rules. If you pass, you move to Phase 2, which usually has a lower profit target (around 5%) and confirms that Phase 1 wasn't a fluke. Pass both, and you receive your funded account.

The key rules throughout are: a maximum daily loss limit (typically 4–5% of the account), a maximum total drawdown limit (typically 8–10%), and in some firms a consistency rule which prevents you from making the bulk of your profit in a single lucky trade. These rules are not there to catch you out — they reflect exactly the discipline a good trader should have anyway.

Challenge fees vary by firm and account size, but typically range from around £50 for a small account to a few hundred pounds for a larger one. Most firms offer a reset or second attempt, sometimes at a discount, if you fail.

Key Concept
💰

Profit Splits & Getting Paid

Click to read
▼

Once you have a funded account, every profitable month results in a payout — your share of the profits you generated on the firm's capital. The split varies by firm but is typically very favourable to the trader: most reputable firms offer between 70% and 90% of profits, with some increasing that share as you build a track record with them.

Payouts are usually processed monthly, though some firms offer more frequent withdrawals once you are established. Payment is typically made via bank transfer or through platforms like Wise or PayPal, depending on the firm.

It is worth understanding what you are and are not entitled to. You are entitled to your agreed profit share on any gains above your starting balance. You are not entitled to the firm's capital itself — you are trading it on their behalf. If you breach the risk rules and the account is closed, you lose access to the funded account but you do not owe the firm any money. Your downside is always limited to the original challenge fee you paid.

Some firms also offer scaling plans — if you hit consistent profit targets over several months, they will increase your account size, giving you access to even more capital as your track record grows.

Key Concept
⚠️

Rules, Risks & Drawdown Limits

Click to read
▼

Every prop firm operates with a set of rules that you must follow throughout your challenge and on your funded account. These rules are not optional — breach any one of them and your account will typically be closed immediately, regardless of how well you have been trading overall. Understanding them before you start is essential.

Maximum Daily Loss Limit. The maximum you are permitted to lose in a single trading day — typically around 4–5% of your account balance. Hit this limit and you are locked out for the rest of the day or, in many firms, the account is failed outright. This means a couple of losing trades in quick succession on a volatile morning can end your challenge before lunchtime.

Maximum Total Drawdown. The overall loss limit across the entire challenge or funded account — typically 8–10% of the starting balance. Once your equity drops by this amount from its peak, the account is closed. As discussed in the Firms card, the total drawdown is the true measure of your available capital, not the headline account size.

Minimum Trading Days. Most firms require you to trade on a minimum number of days — often five to ten — before you can claim a payout or pass a phase. This prevents traders from gambling everything on a single lucky day and calling it skill.

Consistency Rules. Some firms cap how much of your overall profit can come from a single day's trading. If one exceptional day accounts for more than 30–40% of your total profit, it may be flagged and your account reviewed. This is designed to identify consistently disciplined traders rather than one-trade wonders.

Prohibited Strategies. Most firms ban high-frequency scalping, certain forms of arbitrage, and copying trades between accounts. Some also restrict trading around major news events. Always read the small print carefully — what is permitted varies significantly between firms.

The risk nobody talks about enough. Beyond the formal rules, there is a broader risk that deserves honest mention: the emotional pressure of trading under strict constraints is significantly greater than trading freely. Many traders who perform well on demo or personal accounts find that fear of breaching a drawdown limit causes them to manage trades differently — closing winners too early, hesitating on valid entries, or abandoning their strategy under pressure. The rules do not just test your trading ability. They test your psychological resilience, which is why the mindset content in Section 1 matters just as much here as the technical knowledge.

Important
🏆

Firms I've Used & Recommend

Click to read
▼

Choosing a prop firm is an important decision and one worth taking some time over. The industry has grown rapidly and while there are many legitimate, well-run firms, there are also some that are poorly managed or, in the worst cases, not entirely straightforward about their terms. A little research before committing goes a long way.

How prop firms make their money
It is not primarily from funding successful traders — it is from challenge fees. The reality is that the majority of people who attempt a challenge do not pass, and many of those who fail subscribe again and try a second or third time. Those repeated fees add up. For the firms, a high failure and re-subscription rate is a profitable business model. This is not necessarily sinister — the challenges are genuinely difficult and are designed to be — but it is worth being clear-eyed about it. The only money you are ever risking with a prop firm is your challenge fee. That fee may not seem large on its own, but if you attempt challenges multiple times or across different firms, those costs can quietly accumulate into a significant sum. Keep track of what you are spending.

When choosing a firm, look for transparent rules with no hidden clauses, a clear and fair payout process, independently verified reviews, and a firm that has been operating for at least a year or two. Newer firms carry more risk — a small number have shut down without honouring payouts.

⚠️ The most important thing to understand: drawdown limits
The size of a funded account can create a very misleading impression of how much capital you actually have to work with. This is critical, so please read it carefully.

On a personal account, if you have $50,000 and follow the standard rule of risking no more than 1% per trade, your stop loss on any given trade is $500. To lose your entire account you would need to hit that stop loss one hundred times in a row. You have an enormous amount of room to absorb losses while you find your feet.

A prop firm account works very differently. On a $50,000 challenge account, the total maximum drawdown permitted might be as little as $2,500 — and sometimes even less. That means your entire margin for error across all your trades combined is $2,500. If you are still risking 1% per trade ($500), that is only five losing trades before the account is gone. Hit your stop loss two or three times on a bad morning and you can find yourself perilously close to the limit.

The drawdown limit — not the account size — is the true measure of how much capital you have available. Always think in terms of your drawdown allowance, plan your position sizes accordingly, and treat every trade as if it matters far more than it would on a personal account. Because on a prop firm challenge, it does.

Copy Trading

Click to read
▼

Copy trading is a method that allows one person to automatically replicate the trades of another trader in real time. When the trader you are copying opens a position, the same position opens proportionally in your account. When they close it, yours closes too. You do not need to watch the charts, make decisions, or understand why any particular trade was taken. The process is entirely passive once set up.

The appeal is obvious, particularly for beginners still developing their own strategy. Instead of risking money while learning, you can in theory profit by following someone who already knows what they are doing. Several platforms facilitate this, including MetaTrader 5's own signal service and dedicated platforms such as eToro, Myfxbook Autotrade, and ZuluTrade.

However, copy trading comes with significant caveats worth understanding before committing any money to it.

Past performance is not a guarantee of future results. The trader you are copying may have had an excellent six months and then encounter market conditions their strategy is not built for. Their track record tells you what happened, not what will happen.

You are entirely dependent on someone else's discipline. If the trader you follow abandons their risk management, increases their position sizes recklessly, or simply hits a prolonged losing streak, your account suffers alongside theirs. You have no control and no warning.

The numbers can be misleading. Many copy trading platforms display return percentages prominently but do not make drawdown figures equally visible. A trader showing a 200% annual return who also experienced a 60% drawdown at some point is not necessarily someone you want to follow — but you might not notice that detail unless you look carefully.

Copy trading and prop firms do not mix. Most prop firms explicitly prohibit copy trading on their challenge and funded accounts. They are evaluating you as an individual trader, and using a copy trading service to pass a challenge is considered a breach of their rules in most cases. If you are pursuing a prop firm account alongside exploring copy trading, keep them completely separate.

Used with realistic expectations and careful selection of who you follow, copy trading can be a useful way to participate in the markets while you are still building your own skills. But it is not a passive income machine, and approaching it as one is where most people come unstuck.

🤖

What is an Expert Advisor?

Click to read
▼

An Expert Advisor — almost always referred to simply as an EA — is an automated trading program that runs inside MetaTrader 5. Once set up and switched on, it monitors the market on your behalf and can open, manage, and close trades automatically, based on a set of rules you define. You don't need to be sitting at your screen for it to work.

The rules an EA follows can be as simple or as complex as you like. A basic EA might say: "If the 50-period moving average crosses above the 200-period moving average, open a buy trade with a stop loss of 20 pips and a take profit of 40 pips." A more advanced one might factor in multiple indicators, time-of-day filters, news avoidance windows, and dynamic position sizing — all running simultaneously across several pairs.

EAs are valuable for several reasons. They remove emotion from the equation entirely — the EA doesn't panic, doesn't get greedy, and doesn't hesitate. They can monitor the market around the clock, including sessions you are asleep for. And they allow you to test a strategy rigorously on historical data before risking a single penny of real money.

However, EAs are not a magic solution. A poorly designed EA will lose money just as consistently as a good one makes it. The EA is only ever as good as the logic behind it. There is also a risk called over-optimisation — where an EA is tuned so perfectly to historical data that it performs brilliantly in backtests but falls apart in live market conditions. This is covered in more detail in the Important Caveats card.

Used thoughtfully, though, an EA can be a powerful addition to a trader's toolkit — and the good news is that you no longer need to be a programmer to build one.

This matters because writing an EA from scratch in MQL5 — the programming language MetaTrader uses — is genuinely difficult.
